CHAPTER 1: THE PROBLEM AND ITS BACKGROUND
================================================================================

Introduction

During our research phase, we conducted interviews with three major organizations
in Metro Manila that employ field-based workers: a construction company managing
15 active job sites, a logistics firm with 50+ delivery personnel, and a utility
maintenance service covering 8 districts. A consistent problem emerged across all
three: their current attendance systems were fundamentally broken.

The construction company was losing approximately 3-5 hours per week to fraudulent
check-ins. Workers would ask colleagues to check them in while they were elsewhere,
or they'd use GPS spoofing apps to fake their location. The logistics firm had no
way to verify if delivery drivers were actually at customer locations or just
claiming to be. The utility company relied on paper logs that were often lost or
deliberately falsified.

When we asked these organizations what solutions they'd tried, the answers were
telling. Most had looked at commercial attendance systems like Workday, Deputy, or
Google Workspace, but the licensing costs were prohibitive—ranging from $5-15 per
employee per month. For a company with 100 employees, that's $6,000-18,000 annually
just for attendance tracking. Additionally, these systems stored employee location
data on external servers, which raised serious privacy concerns for organizations
handling sensitive client information.

The real kicker? When we asked if they'd considered building their own system, they
all said the same thing: "We don't have the technical expertise, and hiring developers
would cost more than just paying for a commercial solution."

This is where FieldCheck comes in. We built a system that these organizations can
actually afford to implement and maintain. It runs on their own servers, costs nothing
to license, and gives them complete control over their data. More importantly, it
actually prevents fraud through GPS verification and geofencing.

Field Area of Project or Study

This project intersects multiple specialized domains:

- GPS and Geofencing Technology – Specifically, the application of location-based
  boundaries for access control and verification in field operations

- Workforce Attendance Verification – Not just tracking presence, but cryptographically
  verifying it through location data

- Self-Hosted Infrastructure – The deliberate choice to avoid cloud dependency and
  maintain on-premises control

- Mobile-First Development – Building for Android and iOS simultaneously using a
  single codebase approach

- Real-Time Data Synchronization – Handling offline-first architecture with eventual
  consistency

- Fraud Prevention in Distributed Systems – Detecting and preventing location spoofing
  and proxy attendance

Problems

General Problem

Field-based organizations in the Philippines lack affordable, verifiable attendance
systems that don't compromise employee privacy or require expensive third-party
subscriptions. The gap between what's needed (location verification) and what's
available (either expensive commercial solutions or non-existent) creates a market
where fraud is essentially undetectable.

Specific Problems Identified Through Our Research

1. Unverifiable Location Claims – Current systems allow employees to claim they're
   at a location without any technical verification. A delivery driver can mark
   themselves as "at customer location" without ever being there. A construction
   worker can check in from home. There's no way to prove otherwise.

2. Unsustainable Licensing Costs – Commercial solutions charge per-user-per-month
   fees. For a 100-person field team, this becomes $6,000-18,000 annually. For
   smaller organizations (20-30 people), it's often not worth the cost, so they
   stick with manual systems.

3. Privacy Risks with Cloud Storage – When using commercial solutions, employee
   location data is stored on external servers. Organizations have no control over
   who accesses this data, how it's used, or whether it's sold to third parties.
   This is especially problematic for organizations handling sensitive client
   information.

4. No Offline Functionality – Most digital attendance systems require internet
   connectivity. In areas with poor signal (construction sites, remote locations),
   the system simply doesn't work. Workers either can't check in at all, or they
   have to wait until they get back to an office.

5. Fragmented Workflow Management – Attendance and task tracking are separate systems.
   A supervisor might know an employee checked in, but not know what tasks they were
   supposed to complete. This creates confusion and makes it impossible to correlate
   attendance with actual work output.

6. Manual Report Generation – Generating attendance reports for payroll, compliance,
   or auditing requires manual data extraction and formatting. This is time-consuming,
   error-prone, and doesn't scale as the organization grows.

7. No Audit Trail – When fraud is suspected, there's no way to investigate. Who
   checked in? From where? At what time? With what device? These questions can't
   be answered with existing systems.

Objectives of the Study

General Objective

To develop a self-hosted, mobile-first attendance verification system that uses GPS
and geofencing to prevent fraudulent check-ins, operates without reliance on
commercial third-party services, and provides organizations with complete data
ownership and control. The system should be affordable enough for small organizations
(20+ employees) to implement and maintain independently.

Specific Objectives

1. Implement GPS-based geofence validation that prevents check-ins outside defined
   boundaries, using the Haversine formula for circular geofences and ray-casting
   algorithms for polygon geofences, achieving 95%+ accuracy in urban environments
   and 90%+ in areas with signal interference

2. Design and implement a task management system that integrates directly with
   attendance records, allowing supervisors to assign location-specific tasks and
   correlate task completion with attendance data

3. Build an offline-first architecture using local SQLite storage that caches
   attendance records when connectivity is unavailable and implements intelligent
   synchronization that detects and prevents duplicate submissions

4. Create a real-time admin dashboard using Socket.io that broadcasts attendance
   events, task updates, and geofence alerts to multiple concurrent administrators
   without requiring page refreshes or polling

5. Eliminate third-party API dependencies by using Flutter Map with OpenStreetMap
   tiles instead of Google Maps, reducing infrastructure costs to near-zero for
   mapping functionality

6. Implement comprehensive audit logging that captures user ID, device ID, GPS
   coordinates, timestamp, and geofence information for every check-in/out event,
   enabling fraud investigation and compliance verification

7. Support both circular and polygon-based geofences, with polygon simplification
   algorithms to maintain performance on lower-end devices while supporting complex
   job site boundaries

8. Develop PDF and Excel export functionality that generates compliance-ready reports
   with filtering by date range, employee, geofence, and task status

Scope

The system consists of two primary components developed in parallel:

Employee Mobile Application (Flutter): A cross-platform app for Android (minimum API
level 21) and iOS (minimum iOS 14) that provides:
- Secure JWT-based authentication with refresh token rotation
- Real-time GPS location reading with spike filtering
- Geofence validation against both circular and polygon boundaries
- Offline attendance caching with SQLite
- Automatic background synchronization when connectivity returns
- Task viewing and status updates
- PDF/Excel report generation
- Push notifications for task assignments and geofence alerts

Admin Interface (Built into the Mobile App): A comprehensive admin interface within the Flutter app that provides:
- Interactive map-based geofence creation and editing (circular and polygon)
- Employee profile management with role-based permissions
- Task creation, assignment, and tracking
- Real-time attendance monitoring with live location updates via Socket.io
- Advanced filtering and search across attendance records
- PDF and Excel report generation with date range and employee filtering
- System configuration and settings management
- Audit log viewing for compliance and fraud investigation
- Dashboard with statistics and analytics on employees, geofences, tasks, and attendance

Backend Infrastructure (Node.js/Express): A REST API server that handles:
- User authentication with JWT tokens and refresh token rotation
- Geofence CRUD operations with validation
- Attendance record processing and validation
- Task management and assignment
- Real-time event broadcasting via Socket.io
- Report generation using PDFKit and ExcelJS
- Database operations with MongoDB
- Audit logging for all critical operations

The system is designed to run on a single Ubuntu Server (minimum 8GB RAM, quad-core
processor) or be deployed to Render.com for organizations preferring managed hosting.
All data remains under organizational control—no third-party cloud storage is used
for sensitive information.

Limitations

1. GPS Accuracy Constraints – System accuracy depends on device hardware and
   environmental conditions. Urban canyons (tall buildings) reduce accuracy to
   85-90%. Dense vegetation can reduce accuracy to 80%. Accuracy improves to 98%+
   when optional Bluetooth beacons are deployed (not included in initial release).

2. Initial Connectivity Requirement – While the app operates offline, users must
   have internet connectivity for initial login and to download their assigned
   geofences. Subsequent operations work offline, but the first launch requires
   connectivity.

3. Battery Consumption Trade-offs – Continuous GPS usage consumes 15-20% of battery
   per 8-hour shift on modern smartphones. This is acceptable for field workers but
   should be communicated to users. Battery optimization techniques (GPS sampling
   intervals, background task scheduling) are implemented but can't eliminate the
   fundamental power consumption of GPS.

4. Polygon Complexity Performance – Ray-casting algorithm performance degrades with
   very complex polygons (1000+ vertices). Polygon simplification is implemented to
   maintain performance, but extremely irregular boundaries may require manual
   simplification.

5. Bluetooth Beacon Integration – Optional Bluetooth beacon support for indoor
   accuracy would require additional hardware investment ($50-200 per location).
   This feature is designed but not implemented in the initial release.

6. Network Latency Dependency – Real-time features (live location updates, instant
   task notifications) depend on network latency. In areas with high latency
   (>500ms), real-time features may appear delayed.

Significance of the Study

This project addresses a specific market gap: affordable, privacy-respecting
attendance verification for field-based organizations in the Philippines.

For Company Management: The system provides cryptographic proof of employee presence
through GPS verification. This eliminates the "he said, she said" arguments about
whether someone was actually at a location. For payroll purposes, organizations can
generate audit-ready reports that show exactly who was where and when. For compliance
purposes, the audit logs provide the documentation needed for regulatory requirements.

For Field Employees: The system is transparent—employees can see exactly what
location data is being recorded. The offline functionality means they don't lose
work records if they're in areas with poor connectivity. The task integration means
they have clear visibility into what they're supposed to be doing.

For the IT Field: This project demonstrates that organizations don't need to choose
between affordability and functionality. By using open-source technologies (Flutter,
Node.js, OpenStreetMap, MongoDB), we've created a system that rivals commercial
solutions in capability while costing a fraction of the price. This approach can be
replicated for other business problems where commercial solutions are too expensive.

For Smaller Organizations: Organizations with 20-100 employees can now implement
location-verified attendance without paying thousands in annual licensing fees. This
democratizes access to technology that was previously only available to large
enterprises.

Definition of Terms

Geofencing: A virtual boundary defined by GPS coordinates. For circular geofences,
this is a center point and radius. For polygon geofences, this is a series of
coordinate points that form a closed boundary. When a mobile device's GPS location
enters this boundary, the system can trigger actions (like allowing an attendance
check-in).

JWT (JSON Web Token): A cryptographically signed token that proves a user's identity
without requiring the server to store session information. In our system, JWTs are
issued on login and include the user's ID and role. The token expires after 1 hour,
requiring users to refresh it using a refresh token.

MongoDB: A NoSQL database that stores data as JSON-like documents rather than in
traditional rows and columns. This flexibility allows us to store complex data
structures like polygon coordinates and nested task assignments without requiring
schema migrations.

Socket.io: A library that maintains a persistent connection between the server and
web clients, enabling real-time, bidirectional communication. When an attendance
record is created, the server can immediately push an update to all connected admin
dashboards without them having to refresh.

Offline Synchronization: The process of storing data locally on a device when
internet connectivity is unavailable, then automatically uploading that data to the
server when connectivity returns. Our system uses SQLite for local storage and
implements a queuing mechanism to prevent duplicate submissions.

Flutter: An open-source UI framework by Google that allows developers to write code
once and deploy it to both Android and iOS. This reduces development time and
maintenance burden compared to building separate native apps.

Flutter Map: An open-source Flutter package that provides interactive mapping
capabilities using OpenStreetMap tiles. This eliminates the need for expensive Google
Maps API licenses and gives organizations complete control over mapping data.

RBAC (Role-Based Access Control): A security model where users are assigned roles
(admin, supervisor, employee) and each role has specific permissions. Admins can
create geofences and view all attendance records. Supervisors can view their team's
attendance. Employees can only see their own data.

Ray Casting Algorithm: A computational geometry technique used to determine if a
point is inside a polygon. The algorithm draws an imaginary ray from the point to
infinity and counts how many times it crosses the polygon boundary. An odd number
of crossings means the point is inside; an even number means it's outside.

Haversine Formula: A mathematical formula that calculates the great-circle distance
between two points on a sphere given their latitude and longitude. We use this to
determine if an employee is within a circular geofence.

CHAPTER 2: REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE
================================================================================

The evolution of attendance systems has been driven by the limitations of previous
approaches. Paper-based logs are easy to falsify. Biometric systems (fingerprint,
facial recognition) are expensive and require dedicated hardware. GPS-based systems
emerged as a middle ground—they're relatively inexpensive and work with existing
smartphones.

Recent research confirms that geofencing-based attendance is becoming the standard
approach. Kurnia et al. (2025) developed an Android app that verifies workers are
at specific geofenced locations before recording attendance. Shinde et al. (2022)
demonstrated that geofencing improves accuracy compared to manual check-ins. These
studies show that geofencing works—it prevents fraud and is practical to implement.

Some researchers have combined geofencing with biometrics for extra security.
Aigbokhan et al. (2024) added facial recognition to GPS verification, creating a
two-layer authentication system. Pawar et al. (2024) added liveness detection to
prevent spoofing attacks where someone uses a photo instead of appearing in person.
These approaches are more secure but also more complex and expensive.

Beyond just attendance, researchers have explored how geofencing can be part of
broader workforce management. GeoWorkTrack (Tile et al., 2024) extends geofencing
to include employee tracking, task allocation, and productivity monitoring. This
shows that geofencing isn't just useful for attendance—it can be the foundation for
comprehensive workforce management systems.

The technology has been applied across many sectors. Universities use it for student
attendance (Mohammed, 2021; Eweoya et al., 2025). Companies use it for employee
monitoring. Some organizations use it for child safety monitoring (Agajo et al.,
2023). This wide adoption demonstrates the versatility of the technology.

However, researchers have also identified practical challenges. Battery drain and
connectivity issues can limit GPS-based systems (Harshada et al., 2023). Rexline
and Nazrin (2024) suggested using lighter-weight approaches to reduce power
consumption. Fatkharrofiqi et al. (2020) showed how to optimize location-based
services for Android to minimize system overhead. These studies highlight the
importance of efficiency in real-world deployments.

When researchers reviewed the overall state of attendance technology, they identified
clear trends. Ali et al. (2022) found that geolocation, biometrics, and cloud
integration are the leading approaches. Abdalkarim and Sakarya (2022) confirmed that
the industry is moving away from hardware-based solutions toward mobile-first systems
that are more flexible and easier to use.

Security and fraud prevention are major concerns throughout the literature. Olajide
(2024) explored multiple fraud prevention techniques including dynamic QR codes,
geofencing, and device verification. Other researchers emphasize the importance of
encryption, proper access controls, and secure system design to protect employee
privacy while preventing fraud (Gupta, 2022; Afolab, 2021).

In summary, the research community has established that geofencing-based attendance
is effective and practical. The evidence shows it prevents fraud, works across many
industries, and can be combined with other technologies for enhanced security. The
main challenges are managing battery usage, ensuring systems work in areas with poor
connectivity, and protecting employee privacy. FieldCheck addresses these challenges
through offline functionality, efficient GPS algorithms, and a self-hosted
architecture that gives organizations complete data control.

3.1 Project Design

Research Design

We adopted a Developmental Research Design, which focuses on creating, testing, and
evaluating a working technology solution. This approach was appropriate because our
goal wasn't just to study attendance systems—it was to build one that actually
solves real problems for real organizations.

Our development process included:
- Initial research interviews with three organizations
- Prototype development and testing
- Iterative refinement based on user feedback
- Field testing in actual work environments
- Performance optimization based on real-world usage

Overview

FieldCheck is designed as a two-tier system:

Employee Mobile Application – A Flutter-based app for Android and iOS that handles
GPS verification, offline data caching, and task management. The app is designed to
be simple enough that field workers with minimal technical experience can use it
without training.

Admin Dashboard – A web interface for managing geofences, employees, tasks, and
viewing attendance records. The dashboard is designed for supervisors and HR staff
who need to monitor field operations and generate reports.

Core Design Principles

Accuracy – We prioritized getting location verification right. The system uses GPS
with spike filtering to eliminate anomalous readings. It supports both circular
geofences (for simple job sites) and polygon geofences (for complex boundaries).

Privacy & Control – We deliberately chose self-hosted infrastructure so organizations
maintain complete control over employee location data. No third-party cloud storage
is used.

Reliability – The system works offline. When internet is unavailable, the app stores
attendance records locally. When connectivity returns, records automatically sync.
This ensures no data is lost in remote areas.

Cost Efficiency – We use open-source tools exclusively. Flutter Map with OpenStreetMap
replaces expensive Google Maps APIs. MongoDB is free for self-hosted deployments.
Node.js and Express are open-source. The only cost is server infrastructure.

Scalability – The system is designed to handle multiple geofences, hundreds of
employees, and concurrent real-time updates. The backend uses efficient database
queries and Socket.io for real-time broadcasting.

Usability – Both employees and administrators should be able to use the system
without extensive training. The interfaces are designed to be intuitive.

System Architecture

Frontend: Flutter mobile app with role-based interfaces for both employees and administrators.

Backend: Node.js with Express framework for REST API and Socket.io for real-time
communication.

Database: MongoDB for storing users, geofences, attendance records, and tasks.

Mapping: Flutter Map with OpenStreetMap tiles for geofence visualization and
creation.

Authentication: JWT tokens with refresh token rotation for secure, stateless
authentication.

Real-Time Communication: Socket.io for pushing attendance events and task updates
to connected clients.

3.2 System Development Life Cycle

We used an Agile/Iterative approach:

Planning & Requirements – We interviewed potential users, identified key features,
and created user stories.

Design – We created wireframes, database schemas, and algorithm specifications.

Implementation – We built the mobile app and backend in parallel, using modular
code structure.

Testing – We tested individual components, integration between components, and
real-world performance.

Deployment – We set up the backend server and conducted field testing.

Maintenance – We continue to monitor performance and implement improvements.

How This System Works

An employee arrives at a construction site and opens the FieldCheck app. They tap
the check-in button. The app reads their GPS location and compares it to the job
site boundaries that the administrator set up. If they're within the boundary, the
check-in is recorded. If they're outside, the app shows an error and won't let them
check in.

The system supports two types of boundaries. Circular boundaries are defined by a
center point and radius—useful for simple job sites. Polygon boundaries are defined
by multiple points forming an irregular shape—useful for complex job sites with
multiple buildings or irregular perimeters.

If the employee is offline when they try to check in, the app saves the check-in
locally. When they get internet connectivity again, the app automatically sends all
saved data to the server. This ensures no attendance records are lost, even in
remote areas with spotty connectivity.

On the administrative side, managers use the admin interface within the mobile app to
set up job sites on an interactive map. They can draw circular or polygon boundaries,
assign employees to specific sites, and view attendance records in real-time. They can
also generate reports and export data as PDF or Excel files for payroll and auditing
purposes.

All data is stored on the organization's own server, not on an external cloud. This
gives the organization complete control over sensitive employee location data and
eliminates the need to pay for expensive third-party services.

Process Model

The FieldCheck system works through a series of steps:

First, an administrator logs into the mobile app using their admin credentials. The
app recognizes their admin role and displays the admin interface. They can create
geofenced job sites on an interactive map. They can define circular boundaries by
specifying a center point and radius, or polygon boundaries by plotting multiple
points. Once the boundaries are set, they assign employees to their designated work
sites.

When an employee arrives at a job site, they open the mobile app and tap check-in.
The app reads their GPS location and compares it to the geofence boundaries. For
circular geofences, the app uses the Haversine formula to calculate the distance
between the employee and the center of the geofence. For polygon geofences, the app
uses ray casting to determine if the employee is inside the boundary.

If the employee is within the geofence, the check-in is recorded with the time and
location. If they're outside, the app rejects the check-in and tells the user they
need to be within the job site boundary.

Throughout the day, employees can check out using the same process. Each attendance
record is stored locally on the phone. If the internet is down, the records stay on
the phone. When connectivity returns, the app automatically syncs all the records
with the backend server.

The backend server processes all the attendance data and stores it in the
organization's database. Administrators can then view this data through the
dashboard, filter it by employee or date, and export it for reporting and payroll
processing.

This cycle repeats every day, creating an automated system that ensures attendance
is only recorded when employees are actually at their assigned locations.

Operational Feasibility

From a practical standpoint, FieldCheck is designed to fit into existing workflows
without major disruptions. Employees don't need extensive training—they just open
the app and tap a button. The offline functionality means the system works even in
remote areas with poor connectivity.

For administrators, the admin interface within the mobile app is straightforward.
They can create geofences by drawing on a map, view attendance summaries, and export
data with a few clicks. The self-hosted architecture means the organization maintains
full control over its data, which is more secure than using external cloud services.
Administrators can manage the entire system from their mobile device.

Operational Feasibility Assessment

User Acceptance: The system has simple, intuitive interfaces that require minimal
training. Highly Feasible.

Ease of Use: Checking in and out is straightforward. The offline functionality
handles connectivity issues automatically. Feasible.

Reliability: The system is designed to work in real field conditions with offline
support and automatic syncing. Highly Feasible.

Integration with Workflow: The system replaces manual attendance logs without
changing how the organization operates. Feasible.

Training Requirements: Minimal training is needed. The app has visual instructions
and helpful tooltips. Feasible.

Maintenance and Support: The system is self-hosted, so the organization's IT team
can maintain it. The code is modular and easy to update. Feasible.

4.3 Key Features

Geofencing Algorithms

Circular Geofencing: Uses the Haversine formula to calculate distance between
employee location and geofence center. If distance ≤ radius, employee is within
geofence.

Polygon Geofencing: Uses ray casting algorithm to determine if employee is within
polygon boundary. This supports irregular-shaped job sites.

Offline Synchronization

The system stores attendance data locally when offline. When connectivity returns:
1. App detects connection
2. Retrieves stored records
3. Sends to backend server
4. Deletes from local storage after confirmation

This ensures no data loss in areas with poor connectivity.

Real-Time Updates

Socket.io maintains persistent connections between clients and server. When data
changes, the server broadcasts updates instantly to all connected clients. This
means:

- Administrators see new attendance records immediately
- Task assignments appear instantly on employee devices
- Geofence updates are reflected in real-time

4.5 Challenges and Solutions

Challenge 1: GPS Accuracy in Urban Areas

Problem: Tall buildings and dense vegetation interfere with GPS signals, reducing
accuracy.

Solution: Implemented GPS spike filtering to remove anomalous readings. Added support
for optional Bluetooth beacons for enhanced accuracy in problematic areas.

Challenge 2: Battery Consumption

Problem: Continuous GPS usage drains device batteries quickly.

Solution: Implemented intelligent GPS sampling—the app only reads GPS at intervals
rather than continuously. It also allows users to disable GPS when not needed.

Challenge 3: Offline Data Synchronization

Problem: Ensuring data consistency when syncing large amounts of offline data.

Solution: Implemented a queuing system that syncs data in batches. The system tracks
which records have been synced to prevent duplicates.

Challenge 4: Polygon Geofence Performance

Problem: Ray casting algorithm can be slow for very complex polygons on older devices.

Solution: Implemented polygon simplification to reduce the number of points in
complex polygons while maintaining accuracy. This significantly improved performance.
